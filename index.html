Meconium Aspiration Syndrome

At‑Risk Newborn · Pathophysiology · Management | Adele Pillitteri, 7e

Definition & Epidemiology

Meconium Aspiration Syndrome (MAS) – respiratory distress in a newborn caused by aspiration of meconium‑stained amniotic fluid into the lungs. It occurs in approximately 5–10% of deliveries with meconium‑stained fluid, and about 2–5% of those develop severe MAS. In the Philippines, meconium‑stained amniotic fluid is seen in 8–12% of deliveries, with MAS contributing to significant neonatal morbidity and mortality.

📊 Incidence
Meconium passage occurs in 10–20% of all deliveries (higher in post‑term). MAS develops in 2–10% of meconium‑exposed infants. Mortality rate 5–15% with severe MAS.
📌 Philippines Context
Meconium‑stained amniotic fluid is present in ~10% of births; MAS remains a leading cause of neonatal respiratory distress and admission to NICU in tertiary hospitals. Improved obstetric and neonatal care has reduced mortality.
⚠️ Key point: Not all meconium‑exposed infants develop MAS; intrapartum hypoxia and thick meconium increase risk.

Pathophysiology & Risk Factors

⚠️ Risk Factors
• Post‑term pregnancy (>42 weeks)
• Fetal distress / hypoxia
• Maternal hypertension, diabetes, preeclampsia
• Oligohydramnios
• Thick meconium (grade 2‑3)
• Prolonged labor
• Chorioamnionitis
🧬 Pathophysiology
1. Fetal hypoxia → gasping → meconium passage.
2. Aspiration of meconium into airways → airway obstruction (ball‑valve effect, atelectasis, air trapping).
3. Chemical pneumonitis (inflammation).
4. Surfactant inactivation → RDS.
5. Persistent pulmonary hypertension (PPHN) due to vasoconstriction and remodeling.
🔬 Pathophysiology cascade: Hypoxia → meconium passage → aspiration → airway obstruction → air trapping/pneumothorax → inflammation → surfactant dysfunction → V/Q mismatch → hypoxemia → PPHN.

Clinical Manifestations & Diagnosis

  • Respiratory distress at birth: Tachypnea (>60/min), grunting, nasal flaring, retractions, cyanosis, meconium‑stained skin/nails/umbilical cord.
  • Chest auscultation: Coarse crackles, rhonchi, decreased breath sounds (if pneumothorax).
  • Chest X‑ray findings: Patchy infiltrates, hyperinflation (barrel chest), flattening of diaphragm, possible pneumothorax or pneumomediastinum.
  • Blood gas: Hypoxemia, hypercapnia, metabolic acidosis (if severe).

Management (Intrapartum & Neonatal)

👩‍⚕️ Intrapartum (Current NRP guidelines)
• Routine intrapartum suctioning of mouth/nose after delivery of head is NO longer recommended.
• If meconium‑stained fluid and the newborn is NOT vigorous (poor tone, apnea, bradycardia), intubate and suction meconium directly from trachea before positive‑pressure ventilation.
• If vigorous (strong cry, good tone, HR >100), no routine intubation; provide routine care.
🍼 Neonatal Management
• Respiratory support: oxygen, CPAP, mechanical ventilation (optimize oxygenation, prevent barotrauma).
• Surfactant replacement (helps inactived surfactant).
• Inhaled nitric oxide for persistent pulmonary hypertension (PPHN).
• Antibiotics (cover sepsis).
• Thermoregulation, glucose monitoring.
• Chest physiotherapy and suctioning as needed.
Key principle: “Vigorous meconium‑stained infants require no routine intubation; non‑vigorous infants need direct tracheal suction before ventilation.”

Nursing Care & Complications

  • Nursing interventions: • Monitor respiratory status (oxygen saturation, work of breathing).
    • Maintain airway (positioning, suction prn).
    • Administer oxygen/CPAP/mechanical ventilation as ordered.
    • Provide warmth and fluids.
    • Support parents emotionally; explain condition and treatment.
    • Prevent infection (hand hygiene, aseptic technique).
  • Complications: • Pneumothorax (air leak syndrome)
    • Persistent pulmonary hypertension (PPHN)
    • Secondary infection (pneumonia)
    • Chronic lung disease (bronchopulmonary dysplasia)
    • Neurologic injury from hypoxia
🚨 Emergency signs: Sudden deterioration, asymmetrical breath sounds, hypotension, bradycardia → suspect pneumothorax or PPHN.

Long‑Term Outcomes & Prevention

📅 Long‑Term Outcomes
• Most infants recover fully with no residual lung disease.
• Severe MAS may lead to chronic lung disease (BPD), recurrent wheezing, asthma, and increased risk of respiratory infections in early childhood.
• Neurologic sequelae possible if hypoxic‑ischemic injury occurred.
🛡️ Prevention
• Identify post‑term pregnancies and monitor fetal well‑being.
• Induction of labor at 41 weeks reduces meconium passage.
• Intrapartum fetal monitoring to detect distress.
• Avoid post‑term pregnancy >42 weeks.
• NRP guidelines: avoid routine intrapartum suctioning of non‑vigorous infants; use tracheal suction before PPV.
📌 Nursing role in prevention: Promote timely induction, fetal monitoring, and adherence to NRP protocols.
